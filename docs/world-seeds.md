# World Seeds: The 10-Dimension Framework

A world seed is the **ideological substrate** of a fictional world — not the setting, not the plot, but the *way this world thinks*. The cosmological assumptions so fundamental they're never questioned. The conception of time that structures all narrative. The relationship between humans and the divine that determines what kind of aspiration is even possible.

This document explains the ten dimensions and the scholarly traditions that ground them.

---

## Why Ten Dimensions?

The ten dimensions are not arbitrary. They are the categories that comparative mythology and religious scholarship have found to be structurally universal — the questions every world must answer, even if the answers differ radically.

Campbell's monomyth shows that narrative structure is universal; what varies is how each world populates that structure. Eliade's sacred/profane distinction shows that every world distinguishes between the mundane and the sacred, though where they locate the boundary differs. Propp's morphological analysis shows that certain narrative functions appear across cultures regardless of surface content.

The ten dimensions operationalize these insights: they are the variables along which worlds differ, structured so that every value along each dimension is meaningful for character generation.

## The Ten Dimensions

### 1. Cosmogony — How Did This World Begin?

*The logic and cost of creation.*

This is not just backstory. A world's cosmogony determines:
- Whether the world is fundamentally *good* (created by a benevolent force) or *fallen* (created through violence, mistake, or necessity)
- Whether humans are the point of creation or incidental to it
- What the world "owes" to its creators and what the creators owe to it

**Types:**
- **Ex nihilo** (from nothing): Associated with monotheism. God creates freely and the creation is fundamentally separate from the creator. (Abrahamic traditions)
- **Emanation** (flowing from a source): The world flows from the divine as light flows from the sun. Everything participates in the divine. (Neoplatonism, Advaita Vedanta, Daoist cosmology)
- **Cosmic sacrifice**: The world is made from the dismemberment of a primordial being. (Norse: Ymir; Hindu: Purusha; Babylonian: Tiamat). The world is literally made of divine material.
- **Sexual generation**: The world is born from the union of primordial forces. (Egyptian: Nut and Geb; Greek: Gaia and Ouranos)
- **Emergence**: Beings climb through layers of underworld to reach the current world. (Pueblo traditions; Navajo; many First Nations cosmologies)
- **Cyclical destruction/recreation**: The world has been created and destroyed multiple times. (Hindu yugas; Aztec Five Suns; Norse Ragnarök followed by renewal)

**Character implications**: A character from a cosmic sacrifice cosmogony has a different relationship to violence than one from an ex nihilo cosmogony. Sacrifice *creates* in the first world; in the second world, only the Creator creates, and violence is transgression.

### 2. Ontology — What Kinds of Beings Exist?

*The structure of existence, from gods to matter. Can the levels be crossed?*

Every world has an implicit ontological hierarchy. What matters for characters is:
- **Permeability**: Can humans become divine? Can gods become human? Can the dead return?
- **Legitimacy**: What makes a higher level higher — power, knowledge, purity, age?
- **Communication**: How do the levels talk to each other — prayer, ritual, vision, death?

**Greek**: Strict but permeable. Gods are immortal humans; heroes are semi-divine; mortals can be deified after death (Hercules). The permeability is through *arete* — excellence earns elevation.

**Chinese Daoist**: Layered and permeable through cultivation. Mortals can become xian (immortals) through internal alchemy. The bureaucratic heaven mirrors earthly bureaucracy.

**Abrahamic**: Strict and mostly impermeable. The divine/human boundary is ontological, not just quantitative. Only through specific mechanisms (grace, prophecy, incarnation) does the divine cross into the human.

**Hindu**: Extremely layered and cyclically permeable. Every being is working through karma across multiple lives. The apparent levels are temporary positions in a larger journey.

### 3. Time — What Is the Shape of History?

*Linear, cyclical, spiral? Does time have direction? Can it reverse?*

This is the dimension Eliade studied most carefully. His key insight: every culture distinguishes between *profane time* (the ordinary flow of days) and *sacred time* (mythic time, the eternal now of the founding moment). Ritual is the technology for returning to sacred time.

**Types:**
- **Linear progressive**: History moves forward toward a goal. (Abrahamic eschatology; Enlightenment progress; Marxist historical materialism)
- **Cyclical**: History repeats in great cycles. Ages decline from gold to silver to bronze to iron, then reset. (Greek; Hindu yugas; Aztec suns)
- **Spiral**: Each cycle is similar but not identical; there is progress within the larger pattern. (Many shamanic traditions; some Buddhist cosmologies)
- **Mythic eternal present**: True time is not historical at all; it is the time of the founding myth, always present and accessible through ritual. (Most oral traditions; Eliade's *in illo tempore*)
- **No time / time as illusion**: Historical time is māyā; only the eternal Brahman is real. (Advaita Vedanta; some Mahayana Buddhist positions)

**Character implications**: A character from a linear progressive world measures themselves against an ideal future. A character from a cyclical world sees the present as a moment in a vast repetition. These are fundamentally different motivational structures.

---

### 4. Fate & Agency — Who Is Bound by Fate?

*Who can resist fate, how, and at what cost.*

This dimension varies more than almost any other across world traditions:

**Absolute fate**: The Moirai (Greek Fates) cut the thread; even Zeus cannot override it. But the *manner* of meeting fate is within human agency. Oedipus cannot escape killing his father and marrying his mother, but his character determines how he responds to the knowledge.

**Karma as earned fate**: In Hindu and Buddhist cosmology, your current circumstances are the result of past actions. But this is not arbitrary fate — it is perfectly just causation. And it can be changed through current action.

**Tianming (Mandate of Heaven)**: In Chinese thought, the ruler's legitimacy is a form of fate that can be revoked. Individual fate (*ming*) is largely fixed, but the response to it is moral and open.

**Providence**: In monotheistic traditions, God's plan encompasses all events, but within that plan human free will is genuine. The tension between divine omniscience and human freedom is never quite resolved.

**No fate**: Modern Western secular worldview. There is causation but not fate. The future is genuinely open.

---

### 5. Human-Divine Relation — What Are the Gods Like?

*Are gods parents, contractors, predators, or peers?*

Otto's concept of the *numinous* — the experience of the divine as both fascinating and terrifying — is universal. But the *relational structure* between humans and gods varies:

**Parental**: Gods created humans out of love (or at least intention) and take ongoing interest in human welfare. Humans are beloved children. (Some monotheistic framings)

**Contractual**: Gods and humans operate in a system of exchange — sacrifice, prayer, obedience in exchange for protection, fertility, victory. The relationship is transactional. (Most polytheistic systems have elements of this)

**Adversarial**: The gods are indifferent or hostile; human life is a struggle against divine power. Prometheus steals fire *against* Zeus's will. (Elements in Greek, some African traditions)

**Predatory**: The gods require human life — sacrifice is literal feeding. (Aztec; certain readings of Canaanite religion)

**Peer**: Gods and humans differ in power and longevity but not in kind. Humans can become gods; gods can be killed. (Norse; Greek heroes; some shamanic traditions)

**Same**: The divine is within the human. *Atman is Brahman.* There is no fundamental separation. (Advaita Vedanta; some Buddhist positions; Daoist wu wei)

---

### 6. Death & Afterlife — What Does Death Mean?

*Is death the enemy, the threshold, or the teacher?*

**Death as ending**: The secular modern view. Death is final. This is not found in most traditional cosmologies.

**Death as judgment**: Life is a trial; death reveals the verdict. The afterlife is reward or punishment. (Abrahamic; Egyptian Ma'at weighing)

**Death as threshold**: You pass into another form of existence that continues indefinitely. (Greek Hades; Norse Helheim — gray but ongoing)

**Death as return**: The dead return to the source and may be reborn. (Most reincarnation traditions; Celtic rebirth; some African cosmologies)

**Death as initiation**: Dying is required for transformation. The shaman dies and is reborn; the hero descends to the underworld and returns changed. (Shamanic traditions universally; Greek katabasis)

**Death as illusion**: The apparent death of the individual is within the larger life of the whole. (Advaita Vedanta; some Daoist perspectives)

### 7. Core Tension — What Is This World's Fundamental Conflict?

*The conflict that drives all narrative and can never be finally resolved.*

This is the most important dimension for character generation because it is the engine of story.

Every world has a fundamental opposition that its narrative endlessly explores without ever resolving:

- **Greek**: Human excellence (hubris) vs. divine limit (fate). Humans who achieve too much are punished by gods who feel threatened. But the punishment itself generates excellence.
- **Norse**: Order (the Aesir's civilization) vs. chaos (the Jotnar's wilderness). Ragnarök is not the end; it is the inevitable periodic collision of these two principles.
- **Abrahamic**: Human freedom vs. divine sovereignty. The desire to be like God is what caused the Fall; the struggle to return to God without losing freedom drives all of sacred history.
- **Daoist**: Wu wei (non-action, flowing with the Dao) vs. wei (effortful action, imposing the self on the Dao). The world declines when humans impose; flourishes when they yield.
- **Victorian**: Rational order vs. the chaos of modernity. Reason promises to contain it; experience suggests it cannot.

A character is defined by where they stand on their world's core tension — not which side they choose, but how they navigate the irresolvable conflict.

### 8. Aesthetic DNA — What Does This World Feel Like?

*Colors, rhythms, textures, sounds. If this world were music, what key?*

This is the most difficult dimension to specify but the most immediately felt when reading the output. It determines the *texture* of the three output files — how they sound, what they notice, what kind of beauty they recognize.

**Components:**
- **Sensory priorities**: What does this world primarily notice — visual, auditory, olfactory, tactile?
- **Scale**: Grand and cosmic, or intimate and domestic?
- **Tempo**: Urgent and rushing, or slow and inevitable?
- **Key materials**: What objects carry the most meaning in this world?
- **Color palette**: Not literally, but the emotional tone color of the world
- **Musical analogy**: Often the most useful: Greek tragedy = minor-key tragedy with moments of ecstatic release; Norse = drone and percussion, building toward inevitable crescendo; Tang Chinese poetry = a single instrument with long rests

---

### 9. Key Symbols — What Carries the Most Meaning?

*5-7 symbols and the density of significance they carry.*

Every world has a set of objects or images that accumulate so much meaning that they function as portals to the whole world seed. Correctly identifying these symbols is crucial for character generation, because characters from these worlds will think *through* these symbols.

A symbol is not just an image — it's an image that participates in the world's core tension, cosmogony, and aesthetic simultaneously. The Greek wine cup is not just a drinking vessel: it is the medium of Dionysian release, the container of civilized symposium, the offering to the gods, and the occasion for philosophical dialogue. It sits at the intersection of multiple world seed dimensions.

---

### 10. Seed Essence — The World's Breath

*In one paragraph: how does this world think?*

This is the synthesis. Reading the seed essence, you should be able to feel the world — not understand it analytically, but sense its *breath*, the rhythm of its self-understanding.

The seed essence is what character generation uses as the final integrating frame. Every specific trait generated should be consistent with the seed essence — not as a checklist, but as a vibe check.

---

## Using World Seeds for Character Generation

Once a world seed exists, character generation follows naturally:

1. **Find where the character stands on the core tension** — not which side they're on, but how they navigate the irresolvable conflict
2. **Trace their ideological root to specific dimensions** — which cosmogony shapes their self-understanding? Which time conception structures their motivation?
3. **Ground their voice in the aesthetic DNA** — do they sound like the world they come from?
4. **Let the symbols be their thinking tools** — a character from a world where the wine cup carries this much meaning will *think through* the wine cup differently than one from a world where it doesn't

This is what separates a character with genuine roots from a character with a costume.
